Like </span><a href="https://ropercenter.cornell.edu/how-groups-voted-2004" rel>Bush</a><span>, Trump won thanks partly to a surge of votes from Latino and Asian American voters. Like Bush, he’ll win the popular vote — </span><a href="https://x.com/NateSilver538/status/1856331742392545748" rel>probably by a margin of around 1.4 percentage points</a><span> once all votes are counted. He’ll probably come </span><em>just</em><span> short of an outright majority, although it will be close, and Trump’s Electoral College margin was more impressive than Bush’s, who was only one state (Ohio) away from losing to Kerry. </span></p><p><span>Certainly, the mood feels very different than after Trump’s first win in 2016. Democrats have approached the outcome in a more cerebral and analytical way than I was expecting, with </span><a href="https://www.slowboring.com/p/a-common-sense-democrat-manifesto" rel>manifestos</a><span> about a new way forward for the party and </span><a href="https://www.slowboring.com/p/a-common-sense-democrat-manifesto" rel>unapologetic shifts away from “wokeness”</a><span>. And although you can find people who </span><a href="https://presswatchers.org/2024/11/i-blame-the-media/" rel>blame the New York Times</a><span> for the outcome, for the most part, the vibe shift is in the opposite direction, away from academic polemicism and toward constructive strategic conversations about how Democrats can regain their footing for 2026 and beyond.</span></p><h4 class="header-anchor-post">Trump is likely to be unpopular<div class="pencraft pc-display-flex pc-alignItems-center pc-position-absolute pc-reset header-anchor-parent"><div class="pencraft pc-display-contents pc-reset _pubTheme_1msvp_1"><div id="§trump-is-likely-to-be-unpopular" class="pencraft pc-reset header-anchor offset-top"></div><button tabindex="0" type="button" data-href="https://www.natesilver.net/i/151571616/trump-is-likely-to-be-unpopular" class="pencraft pc-reset pencraft _iconButton2_1xogk_655 _iconButtonBase_1xogk_162 _buttonBase_1xogk_1 _buttonNew_1xogk_93 _size_sm_1xogk_137 _priority_secondary_1xogk_42"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="18" height="18" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round" class="lucide lucide-link"><path d="M10 13a5 5 0 0 0 7.54.54l3-3a5 5 0 0 0-7.07-7.07l-1.72 1.71"></path><path d="M14 11a5 5 0 0 0-7.54-.54l-3 3a5 5 0 0 0 7.07 7.07l1.71-1.71"></path></svg></button></div></div></h4><p>And like Bush, Trump will probably have a challenging second term:</p><ul><li><p>Most incumbents are unpopular these days, especially in their second terms. The incumbent party has now lost three presidential elections in a row.</p></li><li><p><span>According to the national exit poll, Trump was elected with a tepid </span><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/elections/2024/general-results/voter-analysis" rel>48 percent favorability rating.</a><span> However, he actually won 9 percent of voters who had an unfavorable view of him. These people may have seen Trump as a superior alternative to Harris but will be less tolerant now that he has no more elections to run in. In the same poll, 44 percent of voters have a </span><em>very</em><span> unfavorable view of Trump, so the ceiling on his popularity is likely to be fairly capped.</span></p></li><li><p><span>Trump’s most likely successor, vice president-elect JD Vance, is </span><a href="https://projects.fivethirtyeight.com/polls/favorability/jd-vance/" rel>also unpopular</a><span>.</span></p></li><li><p><span>Trump’s </span><a href="https://apnews.com/article/trump-inflation-tariffs-taxes-immigration-federal-reserve-a18de763fcc01557258c7f33cab375ed" rel>plan to enact tariffs</a><span> may significantly increase inflation, which we know voters are highly sensitive to.</span></p></li><li><p>Democrats have the more passionate voter base that more reliably turns out in midterms and special elections.</p></li><li><p>When he’s inaugurated on January 20, Trump will be just as old — actually a few months older — than Joe Biden was at the start of his term.</p></li><li><p>And he’s inheriting all of the problems that Biden faced, like the unstable situation in the Middle East.</p></li><li><p><span>Trump will likely emerge with only a very narrow majority in the House, one where his agenda will often be held hostage to the Freedom Caucus. Depending on how the last few races are called, special elections or absences could potentially even put control of the chamber on the line </span><em>before</em><span> 2026.</span></p></li><li><p><span>Trump might not be interested in gutting the welfare state, but some Republicans in Congress will </span><a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2024/oct/30/mike-johnson-healthcare-trump-harris" rel>try</a><span> to — and Democrats can target them in 2026.</span></p></li><li><p><span>And </span><a href="https://www.jstor.org/stable/2111666" rel>thermostatic shifts in public opinion</a><span> will begin to favor Democrats again. At </span><a href="https://kalshi.com/markets/kxpresnomd/democratic-primary-winner" rel>betting markets</a><span>, a group of relatively moderate candidates — Gretchen Whitmer, Josh Shapiro, Mark Cuban, Pete Buttigieg, and Andy Beshear — have a combined 55 percent probability of being the 2028 nominee versus 17 percent for Gavin Newsom, who would be the closest thing to running Harris’s ideas back in a white male package. AOC is the only real left-wing candidate on the board, with a 5 percent chance. The party has some bad habits to overcome, especially the </span><a href="https://x.com/ArmandDoma/status/1856311900507632099" rel>tendency to ostracize people who only halfway agree with them</a><span>. But it’s a relatively rational party — one that was smart enough to recognize that Harris </span><a href="https://www.natesilver.net/p/against-revisionist-history-on-biden" rel>at least gave them a shot</a><span> when Biden didn’t, while also lamenting that an earlier Biden exit </span><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2024/11/08/us/politics/pelosi-harris-biden-open-primary.html" rel>might have given them more options</a><span>. </span></p><p><span>The 2006 and 2008 elections were the high-water mark for </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fifty-state_strategy#:~:text=In%20the%20context%20of%20American,certain%20states%20as%20%22unwinnable%22." rel>Democrats’ “Fifty-state strategy</a><span>,” the idea that the party ought to compete pretty much everywhere, including by nominating moderates who were good fits for red states and districts. Such a strategy is less likely to succeed now because polarization is stronger and “candidate quality” effects are weaker. But that period was also marked by a spirit of inclusiveness, where </span><a href="https://www.natesilver.net/p/not-everyone-who-disagrees-with-you" rel>heterodox liberal centrists</a><span> like me felt welcome in the broad anti-Bush coalition. Their structural disadvantages in the House, Senate and Electoral College are now much less. Let’s take these one at a time.</p><p><em><strong>The Electoral College</strong></em></p><p><span>As </span><a href="https://www.natesilver.net/p/nate-silver-2024-president-election-polls-model" rel>predicted by the model</a><span>, Pennsylvania was the tipping point state</span><span class="footnote-hovercard-target"><a class="footnote-anchor" data-component-name="FootnoteAnchorToDOM" id="footnote-anchor-1-151571616" href="https://www.natesilver.net/p/its-2004-all-over-again#footnote-1-151571616" target="_self" rel>1</a></span><span>. But I’m skeptical because the shrinking Electoral College/popular vote gap can instead be almost entirely explained by what happened in six noncompetitive states. Democrats’ erosion in California, where they won by 29.1 points in 2020 but are headed to “only” a 20.8-point win based on votes counted so far, cost them 0.9 points off their national popular vote margin — even though it didn’t hurt their Electoral College chances at all. By the same logic, Jon Tester (who lost by 7.4) could give things another try in Montana. In Alaska, Mary Peltola looks likely to lose the state’s lone U.S. House seat, though 20 percent of the vote is outstanding and Alaska has ranked-choice voting, which could reverse a small deficit. However, a party generally doesn’t lose its own swing state seats in a typically friendly midterm environment. Thus, Georgia’s Jon Ossoff and Michigan’s Gary Peters are probably in relatively good shape for 2026. There’s more downside risk to the party in 2028, and conditional on a Republican winning the presidency, they’ll almost certainly maintain a GOP Senate. But if Democrats win the presidency, you’d bet on just enough tailwinds to get them the Senate, too — not a cinch, obviously, but at say 60 or 65 percent odds.</p><p>Meanwhile, the complete sweep of Tester and other Democrats out of the solid Trump states does have one hidden benefit to the party: Democrats no longer have a soft underbelly of seats that were likely to fall at the next presidential year. And progressives no longer have Kirsten Sinema or Joe Manchin to complain about.</p><p><span>It’s true that the Senate tipping point is still a bit Republican-leaning: either North Carolina (2.0 points more red than the rest of the country) or Arizona (4.2 points), depending on whether you need 50 seats or 51. Bush\u2019s second term was a disaster, marked by the <a href=\\\"https://www.vox.com/policy-and-politics/2017/1/9/13781088/social-security-privatization-why-failed\\\">failure of Social Security reform</a>, the ongoing quagmires in Iraq and Afghanistan, and Hurricane Katrina. Democrats had a strong 2006 midterm, gaining 33 seats \u2014 including from some of the <a href=\\\"https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/this-post-brought-to-you-by-poker/\\\">bastards who had taken away my poker</a>. And then Barack Obama romped to the largest Electoral College and popular vote win of the 21st Century so far. Progressivism was in the ascent \u2014 until the Tea Party came along in 2010 and whipped Democrats back to reality again.</p><p class=\\\"button-wrapper\\\" data-attrs=\\\"{&quot;url&quot;:&quot;https://www.natesilver.net/subscribe?&quot;,&quot;text&quot;:&quot;Subscribe now&quot;,&quot;action&quot;:null,&quot;class&quot;:null}\\\" data-component-name=\\\"ButtonCreateButton\\\"><a class=\\\"button primary\\\" href=\\\"https://www.natesilver.net/subscribe?\\\"><span>Subscribe now</span></a></p><p>It\u2019s hard not to see the parallels between Bush\u2019s win in 2004 and Donald Trump\u2019s last week. Like <a href=\\\"https://ropercenter.cornell.edu/how-groups-voted-2004\\\">Bush</a>, Trump won thanks partly to a surge of votes from Latino and Asian American voters. Like Bush, he\u2019ll win the popular vote \u2014 <a href=\\\"https://x.com/NateSilver538/status/1856331742392545748\\\">probably by a margin of around 1.4 percentage points</a> once all votes are counted. He\u2019ll probably come <em>just</em> short of an outright majority, although it will be close, and Trump\u2019s Electoral College margin was more impressive than Bush\u2019s, who was only one state (Ohio) away from losing to Kerry.&nbsp;</p><p>Certainly, the mood feels very different than after Trump\u2019s first win in 2016. Democrats have approached the outcome in a more cerebral and analytical way than I was expecting, with <a href=\\\"https://www.slowboring.com/p/a-common-sense-democrat-manifesto\\\">manifestos</a> about a new way forward for the party and <a href=\\\"https://www.slowboring.com/p/a-common-sense-democrat-manifesto\\\">unapologetic shifts away from \u201Cwokeness\u201D</a>. The party is ready to move past the Clintons, the Bidens and the Obamas \u2014 well, unless Michelle Obama decides to run, I guess \u2014 and it would be stunning if there\u2019s any appetite to nominate Harris or Tim Walz again.</p><p>With a clear, undisputed outcome on relatively high turnout \u2014 likely in the range of 156 million votes, just a hair down from 2020 \u2014 there\u2019s less talk about the term-limited Trump being an existential threat to democracy, rhetoric that may have been persuasive to people like me but never resonated with swing voters. And although you can find people who <a href=\\\"https://presswatchers.org/2024/11/i-blame-the-media/\\\">blame the New York Times</a> for the outcome, for the most part, the vibe shift is in the opposite direction, away from academic polemicism and toward constructive strategic conversations about how Democrats can regain their footing for 2026 and beyond.</p><h4>Trump is likely to be unpopular</h4><p>And like Bush, Trump will probably have a challenging second term:</p><ul><li><p>Most incumbents are unpopular these days, especially in their second terms. The incumbent party has now lost three presidential elections in a row.</p></li><li><p>According to the national exit poll, Trump was elected with a tepid <a href=\\\"https://www.foxnews.com/elections/2024/general-results/voter-analysis\\\">48 percent favorability rating.</a> However, he actually won 9 percent of voters who had an unfavorable view of him. These people may have seen Trump as a superior alternative to Harris but will be less tolerant now that he has no more elections to run in. In the same poll, 44 percent of voters have a <em>very</em> unfavorable view of Trump, so the ceiling on his popularity is likely to be fairly capped.</p></li><li><p>Trump\u2019s most likely successor, vice president-elect JD Vance, is <a href=\\\"https://projects.fivethirtyeight.com/polls/favorability/jd-vance/\\\">also unpopular</a>.</p></li><li><p>Trump\u2019s <a href=\\\"https://apnews.com/article/trump-inflation-tariffs-taxes-immigration-federal-reserve-a18de763fcc01557258c7f33cab375ed\\\">plan to enact tariffs</a> may significantly increase inflation, which we know voters are highly sensitive to.</p></li><li><p>Democrats have the more passionate voter base that more reliably turns out in midterms and special elections.</p></li><li><p>When he\u2019s inaugurated on January 20, Trump will be just as old \u2014 actually a few months older \u2014 than Joe Biden was at the start of his term.</p></li><li><p>And he\u2019s inheriting all of the problems that Biden faced, like the unstable situation in the Middle East.</p></li><li><p>Trump will likely emerge with only a very narrow majority in the House, one where his agenda will often be held hostage to the Freedom Caucus. Depending on how the last few races are called, special elections or absences could potentially even put control of the chamber on the line <em>before</em> 2026.</p></li><li><p>Trump might not be interested in gutting the welfare state, but some Republicans in Congress will <a href=\\\"https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2024/oct/30/mike-johnson-healthcare-trump-harris\\\">try</a> to \u2014 and Democrats can target them in 2026.</p></li><li><p>And <a href=\\\"https://www.jstor.org/stable/2111666\\\">thermostatic shifts in public opinion</a> will begin to favor Democrats again. I don\u2019t expect wokeness to make a comeback, but things like anti-immigrant sentiment may decrease, especially if Trump tries to carry out mass deportations.</p></li></ul><p>Mind you, I wouldn\u2019t count on this being a <em>happy</em> time for Democrats. Trump has a lot of unchecked power and a 6-3 Supreme Court majority. But electorally speaking, these are the seeds out of which comebacks are made, and there\u2019s a good chance that Trump is another octogenarian who overreads his mandate and overreaches in all sorts of ways. It was an impressive win, but the country isn\u2019t <em>that</em> different. Trump will get 3 to 4 million more votes than he did in 2020, while Harris will get about 6 million fewer votes than Biden.</p><p class=\\\"button-wrapper\\\" data-attrs=\\\"{&quot;url&quot;:&quot;https://www.natesilver.net/p/its-2004-all-over-again?utm_source=substack&utm_medium=email&utm_content=share&action=share&quot;,&quot;text&quot;:&quot;Share&quot;,&quot;action&quot;:null,&quot;class&quot;:null}\\\" data-component-name=\\\"ButtonCreateButton\\\"><a class=\\\"button primary\\\" href=\\\"https://www.natesilver.net/p/its-2004-all-over-again?utm_source=substack&utm_medium=email&utm_content=share&action=share\\\"><span>Share</span></a></p><p>And unlike in some elections, Democrats are chastened by their loss. At <a href=\\\"https://kalshi.com/markets/kxpresnomd/democratic-primary-winner\\\">betting markets</a>, a group of relatively moderate candidates \u2014 Gretchen Whitmer, Josh Shapiro, Mark Cuban, Pete Buttigieg, and Andy Beshear \u2014 have a combined 55 percent probability of being the 2028 nominee versus 17 percent for Gavin Newsom, who would be the closest thing to running Harris\u2019s ideas back in a white male package. AOC is the only real left-wing candidate on the board, with a 5 percent chance. The party has some bad habits to overcome, especially the <a href=\\\"https://x.com/ArmandDoma/status/1856311900507632099\\\">tendency to ostracize people who only halfway agree with them</a>. But it\u2019s a relatively rational party \u2014 one that was smart enough to recognize that Harris <a href=\\\"https://www.natesilver.net/p/against-revisionist-history-on-biden\\\">at least gave them a shot</a> when Biden didn\u2019t, while also lamenting that an earlier Biden exit <a href=\\\"https://www.nytimes.com/2024/11/08/us/politics/pelosi-harris-biden-open-primary.html\\\">might have given them more options</a>.&nbsp;</p><p>The 2006 and 2008 elections were the high-water mark for <a href=\\\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fifty-state_strategy#:~:text=In%20the%20context%20of%20American,certain%20states%20as%20%22unwinnable%22.\\\">Democrats\u2019 \u201CFifty-state strategy</a>,\u201D the idea that the party ought to compete pretty much everywhere, including by nominating moderates who were good fits for red states and districts. Such a strategy is less likely to succeed now because polarization is stronger and \u201Ccandidate quality\u201D effects are weaker. But that period was also marked by a spirit of inclusiveness, where <a href=\\\"https://www.natesilver.net/p/not-everyone-who-disagrees-with-you\\\">heterodox liberal centrists</a> like me felt welcome in the broad anti-Bush coalition. Losing the popular vote casts a pall: Democrats can\u2019t even <a href=\\\"https://www.nytimes.com/2024/06/15/upshot/election-democrats-republicans-turnout-trump.html\\\">tell themselves the myth</a> anymore that they\u2019d be in the majority if everybody voted, not in an electorate where Trump turns out the more marginal voters. The party basically has no choice but to hang a HELP WANTED sign outside of DNC headquarters.</p><h4>A level electoral playing field</h4><p>There was also some hidden good news for Democrats in last week\u2019s results. Their structural disadvantages in the House, Senate and Electoral College are now much less. Let\u2019s take these one at a time.</p><p><em><strong>The Electoral College</strong></em></p><p>As <a href=\\\"https://www.natesilver.net/p/nate-silver-2024-president-election-polls-model\\\">predicted by the model</a>, Pennsylvania was the tipping point state<a class=\\\"footnote-anchor\\\" data-component-name=\\\"FootnoteAnchorToDOM\\\" id=\\\"footnote-anchor-1\\\" href=\\\"#footnote-1\\\" target=\\\"_self\\\">1</a>. Its vote count isn\u2019t quite finalized yet, but Trump leads there by 2.0 percentage points. If my projection of a Trump +1.4 popular vote is correct, that means the tipping point is only 0.6 percentage points to the right of the popular vote. Michigan and Wisconsin are now <em>left </em>of the tipping point, meanwhile,&nbsp;</p><p>And Georgia \u2014 even though it flipped from a Biden win to a Trump win \u2014 actually moved considerably toward the tipping point: the Atlanta suburbs and exurbs are <a href=\\\"https://x.com/NateSilver538/status/1856123072644092150\\\">one of the few places</a> where Harris gained ground relative to Biden. Their gains in Florida alone were responsible for 0.7 points of national vote swing, and Texas another 0.6. These six states then \u2014 the four blue states plus the two red ones \u2014 combined to reduce the Electoral College penalty for Democrats by 3.3 points, wiping it out almost entirely. A week later, though, the situation looks considerably better for Democrats than it did on Election Night, with the party securing narrow holds in battlegrounds Arizona, Nevada, Michigan and Wisconsin \u2014 though losing Pennsylvania. That\u2019s still a 53-47 majority for Trump \u2014 importantly, one that\u2019s big enough to withstand defections from Susan Collins and Lisa Murkowski. Still, there\u2019s a path back for Democrats. Let\u2019s break the incoming Senate into three broad categories.</p><ul><li><p><strong>Harris states (38 seats).</strong> Democrats control 37 of 38 seats, with Maine (Susan Collins) as the lone exception.</p></li><li><p><strong>2024 battleground states (14 seats)</strong>. Democrats control 10 of the 14 seats, the exceptions being one of the Wisconsin seats (Ron Johnson), one of the Pennsylvania seats (where David McCormick is Senator-elect) and both North Carolina seats. Of these, 5 will <em>not</em> come up for re-election again until 2030, meaning they\u2019re locked in place for whoever is chosen president in 2028. Democrats control 4 of those 5 seats \u2014 the races they just won in Wisconsin, Michigan, Nevada and Arizona. The GOP controls only McCormick\u2019s seat in Pennsylvania.</p></li><li><p><strong>Solid Trump states (48 seats).</strong> Republicans now control all 48 seats, following Democratic losses in Ohio, Montana and West Virginia.</p></li></ul><p>So, Democrats have a low ceiling in the Senate, while those 48 senators in deep red states give Trump a nice high floor. Democrats will have to try to compete in some of them. The best target is probably Ohio, which will hold a special election in 2026 as Vance advances to the vice presidency. Sherrod Brown lost his race by only 3.8 points in an R +1.4 national environment this year. In a typical midterm environment that favors the \u201Cout\u201D party \u2014 say, a D +5 or D +6 map \u2014 he could probably be competitive if he wants to run again. By the same logic, Jon Tester (who lost by 7.4) could give things another try in Montana. In Alaska, Mary Peltola looks likely to lose the state\u2019s lone U.S. House seat, though 20 percent of the vote is outstanding and Alaska has ranked-choice voting, which could reverse a small deficit. However, she won statewide in the last midterm, 2022. It\u2019s one more option for Democrats against the <a href=\\\"https://morningconsult.com/senator-rankings/?utm_source=email&amp;utm_medium=cta&amp;utm_campaign=2019_Q4_Senator_Rankings\\\">relatively unpopular Dan Sullivan</a>, although Peltola could be hurt if <a href=\\\"https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2024/11/05/us/elections/results-alaska.html\\\">Alaskans repeal ranked-choice voting</a>. However, a party generally doesn\u2019t lose its own swing state seats in a typically friendly midterm environment. Thus, Georgia\u2019s Jon Ossoff and Michigan\u2019s Gary Peters are probably in relatively good shape for 2026. There\u2019s more downside risk to the party in 2028, and conditional on a Republican winning the presidency, they\u2019ll almost certainly maintain a GOP Senate. Sonia Sotomayor <a href=\\\"https://www.natesilver.net/p/sonia-sotomayors-retirement-is-a\\\">should probably have listened to us and retired</a> \u2014 but she may get bailed out if Democrats can finally knock off Collins or turn narrow losses in North Carolina into narrow wins.&nbsp;</p><p><em><strong>The House</strong></em></p><p>That the House is still uncalled in an election where Trump won the popular vote is a favorable sign for Democrats.
